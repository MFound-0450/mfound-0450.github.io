Determinant and Trace

In linear algebra, the determinant and trace are fundamental concepts associated with square matrices. These concepts play a critical role in understanding the properties of matrices, including their invertibility, eigenvalues, and geometric interpretations. In this lesson, we will explore the definition, computation, and properties of the determinant and trace of matrices.

Determinant of a Matrix

The determinant is a scalar value associated with a square matrix. It is denoted as \( \text{det}(\mathbf{A}) \) or \( |\mathbf{A}| \) for a matrix \( \mathbf{A} \). The determinant provides important information about the matrix, such as whether it is invertible and its relation to the volume of a geometric figure in higher dimensions.

Invertibility and Rank

A square matrix \( \mathbf{A} \) is invertible (non-singular) if and only if its determinant is non-zero:

\( \mathbf{A} \text{ is invertible } \iff \text{det}(\mathbf{A}) \neq 0 \)

Additionally, the determinant of \( \mathbf{A} \) is non-zero if and only if the rank of \( \mathbf{A} \) is \( n \), where \( n \) is the size of the matrix.

Determinant of a \( 2 \times 2 \) Matrix

For a \( 2 \times 2 \) matrix \( \mathbf{A} \) given by:

\( \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \)

the determinant is computed as:

\( \text{det}(\mathbf{A}) = ad - bc \)

Determinant of a \( 3 \times 3 \) Matrix

For a \( 3 \times 3 \) matrix \( \mathbf{A} \) given by:

\( \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{bmatrix} \)

the determinant is computed as:

\( \text{det}(\mathbf{A}) = a(ei - fh) - b(di - fg) + c(dh - eg) \)

This formula expands the determinant in terms of the elements of the first row and the corresponding \( 2 \times 2 \) determinants.

General Determinant Calculation (Laplace Expansion)

For an \( n \times n \) matrix, the determinant can be computed using the Laplace expansion, which involves expanding the determinant along a row or a column. The determinant of \( \mathbf{A} \) along column \( j \) is given by:

\( \text{det}(\mathbf{A}) = \sum_{k=1}^{n} (-1)^{k+j} a_{kj} \text{det}(\mathbf{A}_{k,j}) \)

where \( \mathbf{A}_{k,j} \) is the \( (n-1) \times (n-1) \) submatrix obtained by removing the \( k \)-th row and \( j \)-th column from \( \mathbf{A} \).

Similarly, the determinant along row \( i \) is:

\( \text{det}(\mathbf{A}) = \sum_{k=1}^{n} (-1)^{i+k} a_{ik} \text{det}(\mathbf{A}_{i,k}) \)

This method generalizes the calculation of determinants to matrices of any size.

Properties of the Determinant

The determinant has several important properties:

  • Linearity: The determinant is linear with respect to rows and columns.
  • Multiplicative Property: For any two square matrices \( \mathbf{A} \) and \( \mathbf{B} \) of the same size, the determinant of their product is the product of their determinants:

    \( \text{det}(\mathbf{AB}) = \text{det}(\mathbf{A}) \cdot \text{det}(\mathbf{B}) \)

  • Transpose: The determinant of a matrix is equal to the determinant of its transpose:

    \( \text{det}(\mathbf{A}^T) = \text{det}(\mathbf{A}) \)

  • Inverse: If \( \mathbf{A} \) is invertible, the determinant of the inverse is the reciprocal of the determinant:

    \( \text{det}(\mathbf{A}^{-1}) = \frac{1}{\text{det}(\mathbf{A})} \)

  • Effect of Row Operations:
    • Swapping two rows (or columns) multiplies the determinant by \( -1 \).
    • Multiplying a row (or column) by a scalar \( c \) multiplies the determinant by \( c \).
    • Adding a multiple of one row (or column) to another row (or column) does not change the determinant.

Determinant as Volume

The determinant of an \( n \times n \) matrix can be interpreted geometrically as the signed volume of the \( n \)-dimensional parallelepiped formed by the column vectors of the matrix. For example:

  • In 2D, the absolute value of the determinant represents the area of the parallelogram formed by the column vectors.
  • In 3D, the absolute value of the determinant represents the volume of the parallelepiped formed by the column vectors.

The sign of the determinant indicates the orientation of the vectors: a positive determinant means the vectors follow a right-hand rule, while a negative determinant indicates a left-hand rule.

Trace of a Matrix

The trace of a square matrix \( \mathbf{A} \), denoted as \( \text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) \), is the sum of its diagonal elements:

\( \text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_{ii} \)

where \( a_{ii} \) are the diagonal elements of \( \mathbf{A} \).

Properties of the Trace

The trace has several useful properties:

  • Linearity:

    \( \text{tr}(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B}) = \text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) + \text{tr}(\mathbf{B}) \)

  • Homogeneity:

    \( \text{tr}(c\mathbf{A}) = c \cdot \text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) \)

    where \( c \) is a scalar.
  • Cyclic Property:

    \( \text{tr}(\mathbf{AB}) = \text{tr}(\mathbf{BA}) \)

    This property is particularly important in simplifying expressions involving products of matrices.
  • Trace of Identity Matrix:

    \( \text{tr}(\mathbf{I}) = n \)

    where \( \mathbf{I} \) is the identity matrix of size \( n \times n \).
  • Similarity Invariance: For similar matrices \( \mathbf{A} \) and \( \mathbf{B} \), where \( \mathbf{B} = \mathbf{S}^{-1} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{S} \), the traces are equal:

    \( \text{tr}(\mathbf{B}) = \text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) \)

Characteristic Polynomial

The characteristic polynomial of a square matrix \( \mathbf{A} \) is a polynomial which has the eigenvalues of \( \mathbf{A} \) as its roots. It is given by:

\( p(\lambda) = \text{det}(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) \)

where \( \lambda \) is a scalar and \( \mathbf{I} \) is the identity matrix of the same size as \( \mathbf{A} \).

Coefficients of the Characteristic Polynomial

The coefficients of the characteristic polynomial can be expressed in terms of the determinant and trace of the matrix:

  • The constant term is the determinant of the matrix:

    \( c_0 = \text{det}(\mathbf{A}) \)

  • The coefficient of the \( \lambda^{n-1} \) term is the trace of the matrix:

    \( c_{n-1} = (-1)^{n-1} \text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) \)

The characteristic polynomial plays a key role in determining the eigenvalues of the matrix, which are the roots of this polynomial.
