Linear Mappings and Affine Spaces

Vectors can be added together and multiplied by scalars such that the result is also a vector. This is called a linear combination of vectors. For any two vector spaces V and W, a mapping Φ : V → W is called a linear mapping if, for all x, y ∈ V and real-valued scalars, \(a\) and \(b\),

\[\Phi(a\mathbf{x} + b\mathbf{y}) = a\Phi(\mathbf{x}) + b\Phi(\mathbf{y})\]

and \(\Phi(a\mathbf{x} + b\mathbf{y})\), \(a\Phi(\mathbf{x}) + b\Phi(\mathbf{y})\) ∈ W.

Linear mappings can also be represented using matrices, which we call transformation matrices. Linear mappings can also be referred to as vector space homomorphisms or linear transformations.

Vector spaces can be represented using their basis vectors such that every vector in the vector space, obtainable from a linear combination of the basis, is represented using coordinates, which are scalars by which the basis vectors are linearly combined. For a vector space V with ordered basis B = \(\{\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n\}\), any vector x ∈ V can be obtained with a linear combination

\[\mathbf{x} = a_1\mathbf{b}_1 + a_2\mathbf{b}_2 + \dots + a_n\mathbf{b}_n\]

where the scalars \(a_1, \dots, a_n\) are the coordinates of x.

Transformation Matrices

With the coordinate representation of vectors, consider a linear mapping Φ : V → W with ordered basis B = \(\{\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_m\}\) of V and ordered basis C = \(\{\mathbf{c}_1, \mathbf{c}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{c}_n\}\) of W. Each basis vector of V can be mapped to W such that the result is a linear combination of the ordered basis of W. That is,

\[ \begin{align} \Phi(\mathbf{b}_1) &= a_{11}\mathbf{c}_1 + a_{12}\mathbf{c}_2 + \dots + a_{1n}\mathbf{c}_n \notag \\ \Phi(\mathbf{b}_2) &= a_{21}\mathbf{c}_1 + a_{22}\mathbf{c}_2 + \dots + a_{2n}\mathbf{c}_n \notag \\ &\vdots \notag \\ \Phi(\mathbf{b}_m) &= a_{m1}\mathbf{c}_1 + a_{m2}\mathbf{c}_2 + \dots + a_{mn}\mathbf{c}_n \tag{1} \end{align} \]

For any vector x ∈ V, x can be expressed using the ordered basis B of V as

\[\mathbf{x} = a_1\mathbf{b}_1 + a_2\mathbf{b}_2 + \dots + a_m\mathbf{b}_m\]

Applying the linear mapping to x,

\[ \begin{align} \Phi(\mathbf{x}) &= \Phi(a_1\mathbf{b}_1 + a_2\mathbf{b}_2 + \dots + a_m\mathbf{b}_m) \notag \\ &= a_1\Phi(\mathbf{b}_1) + a_2\Phi(\mathbf{b}_2) + \dots + a_m\Phi(\mathbf{b}_m) \notag \\ &= (a_1a_{11} + a_2a_{21} + \dots + a_ma_{m1})\mathbf{c}_1 \notag \\ &\quad + (a_1a_{12} + a_2a_{22} + \dots + a_ma_{m2})\mathbf{c}_2 \notag \\ &\quad + \dots \notag \\ &\quad + (a_1a_{1n} + a_2a_{2n} + \dots + a_ma_{mn})\mathbf{c}_n \tag{2} \end{align} \]

Let the coordinate vector of x be \(\hat{x}\)

\[\mathbf{\hat{x}} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ \vdots \\ a_m \end{bmatrix} \]

The coordinate vector for \(\Phi(\mathbf{x})\) from equation (2) is

\[ \begin{bmatrix} a_1a_{11} + a_2a_{21} + \dots + a_ma_{m1} \\ a_1a_{12} + a_2a_{22} + \dots + a_ma_{m2} \\ \vdots \\ a_1a_{1n} + a_2a_{2n} + \dots + a_ma_{mn} \end{bmatrix} \]

This coordinate vector is obtainable as

\[ \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{21} & \dots & a_{m1} \\ a_{12} & a_{22} & \dots & a_{m2} \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ a_{1n} & a_{2n} & \dots & a_{mn} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ \vdots \\ a_m \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1a_{11} + a_2a_{21} + \dots + a_ma_{m1} \\ a_1a_{12} + a_2a_{22} + \dots + a_ma_{m2} \\ \vdots \\ a_1a_{1n} + a_2a_{2n} + \dots + a_ma_{mn} \end{bmatrix} \]

This is a product of a transformation matrix and the coordinate vector of x. We can denote this transformation matrix by AΦ. Thus, the coordinate vector of \(\Phi(\mathbf{x})\) with respect to the ordered basis of W can be obtained as AΦ \(\hat{x}\).

\[ \mathbf{A}_\Phi = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{21} & \dots & a_{m1} \\ a_{12} & a_{22} & \dots & a_{m2} \\ \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ a_{1n} & a_{2n} & \dots & a_{mn} \end{bmatrix} \]

It can be observed that the columns of AΦ are the coordinate vectors obtained by applying the linear mapping to the ordered basis of V, as seen in equation (1). AΦ is thus the transformation matrix that maps coordinates from V with respect to the ordered basis B to coordinates in W with respect to the ordered basis C.

Change of Basis

For a vector space V with ordered basis B = \(\{\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n\}\), and a vector x ∈ V represented using coordinate vectors with respect to B, we can represent x with respect to a different ordered basis C = \(\{\mathbf{c}_1, \mathbf{c}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{c}_n\}\). For this, we would need a change of basis transformation matrix, similar to the one obtained above.

We define an identity linear transformation Φ : V → V, such that \(\Phi(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}\) that maps x in V with respect to B to x in V with respect to C.

\[\mathbf{x} = a_1\mathbf{b}_1 + a_2\mathbf{b}_2 + \dots + a_n\mathbf{b}_n\]

with coordinates \(\mathbf{\hat{x}}\) with respect to B

\[\mathbf{\hat{x}} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ \vdots \\ a_n \end{bmatrix}\]

The basis vectors are transformed as

\[ \begin{align} \Phi(\mathbf{b}_1) = a_{11}\mathbf{c}_1 + a_{12}\mathbf{c}_2 + \dots + a_{1n}\mathbf{c}_n = \mathbf{b}_1 \tag{3} \\ \Phi(\mathbf{b}_2) = a_{21}\mathbf{c}_1 + a_{22}\mathbf{c}_2 + \dots + a_{2n}\mathbf{c}_n = \mathbf{b}_2 \tag{4} \\ \vdots \notag \\ \Phi(\mathbf{b}_n) = a_{n1}\mathbf{c}_1 + a_{n2}\mathbf{c}_2 + \dots + a_{nn}\mathbf{c}_n = \mathbf{b}_n \tag{5} \\ \end{align} \]

Equations (3) to (5) can be solved to obtain the coefficients \(a_{ij}\) for \(i,j = 1, 2, \dots, n\)

The change of basis matrix that transforms coordinates from B to C is then given as

\[\mathbf{S} = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{21} & \dots & a_{n1} \\ a_{12} & a_{22} & \dots & a_{n2} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{1n} & a_{2n} & \dots & a_{nn} \end{bmatrix} \]

And that which transforms coordinates from C to B is the inverse of S, denoted S-1.

Now consider the linear mapping Φ : V → W with ordered bases B and B' of V, and C and C' of W. A transformation matrix of Φ with respect to B and C, AΦ, can be modified into another transformation matrix with respect to B' and C', A'Φ as follows:

\[\mathbf{A}_\Phi' = \mathbf{T}^{-1} \mathbf{A}_\Phi \mathbf{S}\]

where S is the basis change matrix from B' to B, and T is the basis change matrix from C' to C.

Equivalence and Similarity of Matrices

Two matrices A and B ∈ \(\mathbb{R}^{m \times n}\) are equivalent if there exist regular matrices T ∈ \(\mathbb{R}^{m \times m}\) and S ∈ \(\mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) such that

\[\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{T}^{-1} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{S}\]

Two matrices A and B ∈ \(\mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) are similar if there exists a regular matrix S ∈ \(\mathbb{R}^{n \times n}\) such that

\[\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{S}^{-1} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{S}\]

Image and Kernel

In simple terms, the image of a linear mapping is the vector subspace containing all vectors obtainable from the linear mapping, while the kernel (or null space) of a linear mapping is the vector subspace containing all vectors that are mapped to the zero vector by the linear mapping.
